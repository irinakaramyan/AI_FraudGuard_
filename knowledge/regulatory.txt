TITLE: Regulatory Compliance and Anti-Fraud Frameworks
CATEGORY: regulatory

## Bank Secrecy Act (BSA) / Anti-Money Laundering (AML)
The BSA requires financial institutions to assist government agencies in detecting and preventing money laundering.
Key requirements:
- File Currency Transaction Reports (CTR) for cash transactions exceeding $10,000
- File Suspicious Activity Reports (SAR) within 30 days of detecting suspicious activity
- Maintain records of transactions for 5 years
- Implement a Customer Identification Program (CIP)
- Conduct ongoing customer due diligence (CDD) and enhanced due diligence (EDD) for high-risk customers

SAR filing triggers relevant to this system:
- Transactions with no apparent lawful purpose
- Structuring transactions to avoid reporting thresholds
- Rapid movement of funds (layering)
- Transactions inconsistent with customer's business profile
- Transactions involving known high-risk jurisdictions

## Payment Card Industry Data Security Standard (PCI DSS)
PCI DSS is the global security standard for organizations handling branded payment cards.
Relevant requirements for fraud detection systems:
- Requirement 10: Log and monitor all access to network resources and cardholder data
- Requirement 11: Regularly test security systems and processes
- Requirement 12: Maintain an information security policy
- Requirement 6.4.3: All fraud monitoring systems must be included in vulnerability management
- Fraud detection systems must maintain audit logs for minimum 12 months

## General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR) / CCPA
Privacy regulations affecting how transaction data is stored and processed.
Key considerations:
- Transactions contain PII — must be encrypted at rest and in transit
- Purpose limitation: data collected for fraud detection cannot be used for marketing
- Data minimization: store only what is necessary for fraud detection
- Right to erasure: customer may request deletion (balance against 5-year AML retention)
- Automated decision-making: customers have right to explanation for fraud-based account restrictions

## Financial Action Task Force (FATF) Recommendations
International standards for combating money laundering and terrorist financing.
Relevant recommendations:
- Recommendation 10: Customer due diligence (know your customer - KYC)
- Recommendation 16: Wire transfer information requirements
- Recommendation 20: Suspicious transaction reporting
- High-risk countries on FATF grey/black list should trigger enhanced monitoring

## Fraud Liability and Chargeback Rules
Visa/Mastercard dispute resolution frameworks:
- Merchants bear liability for CNP fraud in most cases (EMV liability shift)
- Financial institution bears liability for card-present fraud on EMV-enabled terminals
- Friendly fraud (customer disputes legitimate charge) is the fastest growing fraud category
- Chargeback thresholds: Visa VAMP program targets merchants exceeding 0.9% chargeback ratio
- Time limits: Most chargebacks must be filed within 120 days of transaction

## Recommended Monitoring Thresholds (Regulatory Alignment)
- Flag any transaction or series of transactions over $10,000 (CTR alignment)
- Flag structuring patterns: multiple transactions just under $10,000 within 24 hours
- Flag wire transfers to FATF non-compliant jurisdictions regardless of amount
- Flag dormant accounts (>90 days inactive) with sudden high-value activity
- Flag new accounts (opened <30 days) with transactions exceeding $2,500

## Customer Risk Classification (CDD Framework)
Low Risk: Retail customers with established history, domestic transactions, salaried employment
Medium Risk: Self-employed, private banking clients, cash-intensive businesses
High Risk: PEPs (Politically Exposed Persons), customers from high-risk jurisdictions, complex ownership structures, history of suspicious activity
The system assigns customer risk levels which influence the ML model's fraud probability calculation.
